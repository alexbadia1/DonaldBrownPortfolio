Why I Am Running

Professional Career

              My career in private practice has been, until the last few years, rewarding, stimulating, gratifying and often joyful. We had a firm which was a lot like family. We had a number of employees who worked for us for 20-30 years. It was a great place to work! I loved practicing law for most of my career. I always considered myself so fortunate to have almost stumbled into a career that I was good at, and that actually helped people and that I loved to do! I have tried jury cases literally from coast-to-coast and even in a foreign country (Israel in 1992). I have represented some absolutely wonderful clients; many of whom became life-long friends. I have represented movie stars, professional athletes and I have regularly represented, throughout my career, persons who could not afford an attorney on a pro bono basis. Several of them have become life-long friends, as well.
              I was educated and trained in the law by some wonderful, wonderful teachers. Those I served with early in my career belong to different political parties; but that is of absolutely no matter. Some are still-sitting federal judges that I went to school with. Some are recently retired Judges from whom I learned much while working side-by-side with them in the D.A.’s Office (Al Rosenblatt, George Marlow, Jerry Hayes, Tom Dolan, Steve Greller , Jerry Pisanelli, Jim Brands, Ira Pergament, Joe Spiegel). I am “old school”. I was taught and I believe that lawyers are public servants. I was taught and I believe that the courts do not exist for the government. The courts exist and derive their authority to serve the people; that they administer the law for the people not for the government.

I was fortunate to serve my profession and my community in many voluntary capacities and I enjoyed them all (or I’ve purged from my memory those I didn’t). Among them:

  • President of the Dutchess County Bar Assn.
  • Member of the NYS Bar Assn . House of Delegates
  • Chairman, Judiciary Comm. Dutchess County Bar Assn.
  • Member, Grievance Comm. Dutchess County Bar Assn.
  • Member, Pro Bono Panel, Legal Services Corp. of the Hudson Valley
  • Member, Pro Bono Panel, Dutchess County Bar Assn.
  • Member, Board of Directors, Dutchess Golf & Country Club
  • Member, Board of Directors, St. Francis Health Care Foundation
  • Member, Board of Directors, Joseph Barnard Memorial County Law Library
  • Member, Board of Directors, Hyde Park Festival Theatre
  • Member, N.Y.S. Comm. on Lawyer Alcoholism and Substance Abuse
  • Member, N.Y.S. Lawyer Assistance Program
  • Coach, Town of Poughkeepsie Little League.

              Almost 36 years ago, following the sudden deaths of my 14-year old daughter and my father, I entered a treatment program for recovery for my then untreated alcoholism. Since that time, I have remained an active member/participant in that recovery program and I have remained completely abstinent from alcohol and drugs since that time. I have served for almost 20 years on the NYS Bar Assn Comm. on Lawyer Alcoholism and Substance Abuse (now “Lawyers Assistance Program”) and have spoken with and counseled, at the request of the State and local bar associations, groups of lawyers and individual lawyers on this subject. More importantly, I have worked with, and continue to do so regularly, men of all ages and backgrounds, and their families, who are recovering/struggling to recover from alcoholism/addiction.
              In April of 2018 I decided to retire from the practice of law. The decision was not an easy one; but it was 4-5 years in coming. As a trial lawyer, I had to deal with the government and the insurance industry virtually every day. The system which I grew up in is now broken and the way that lawyers are trained today is not the way they were trained formerly. As I said, I am “old school”. It seems that the government and the courts perceive that they exist for the institutions themselves and not for the people for whom they should be serving. I saw far too many meritorious cases become bogged down in meaningless delays for endless discovery requests. Court orders were ignored because judges didn’t want to lean too hard on big companies with big law firms; so the system just started grinding to a halt. My clients had no practical remedy because the cost of litigation was skyrocketing. It was demoralizing to tell my clients the truth about this system and have them look at me with eyes that said: “Well, you’re a part of this system, aren’t you?” I couldn’t come up with a good response to that; so I had to leave. I couldn’t just stay around any longer, just for the money.
              I kept my license in case I could use it one day to be of some service. I’m big on service. The local Democratic Committee asked me to consider running. I thought about; and, candidly, my first reaction was: ‘What do I want to do that for? I’m enjoying my 3 wonderful granddaughters and visiting and spending time with old friends and family who live all over the country.” Then after conferring with my fiancé’ and my son (also a lawyer) and giving it some more thought, I felt that I certainly know the job and I know that I have the best qualifications and plenty of time to do the job right. I also know that those now in the job are there because they’ve been holding political office in the town for 25-40 years. To me, that’s not what public service is supposed to be about.
              So…..I feel that this is what I’m called to do, to be of service now; and I pledge my full commitment to the citizens of the town to do the job right and to give it all the time and energy it takes. I am not committed to getting myself off the bench and out of there each day, as quickly as I can, to get back to my personal business or law practice.


Early Years

              I am the son and grandson of NYC Firemen. My great uncle, Jim was a Battalion Chief in the FDNY when I was a kid. I am the oldest of 6, born in Brooklyn and attended school on Long Island. I first came to Poughkeepsie in 1964 to attend Marist College graduating in 1968. While there, I always say “I got a great education despite myself”. studied extremely hard my first and last years; and in the middle, I was involved in music and football (not so much academics). I worked part-time throughout college and full-time at IBM at nights in my senior year in, what was then, the South Road Lab. By the time I graduated, I had married a Poughkeepsie girl and we had a new-born baby daughter. I had always assumed that I would be entering the service upon graduation (as this was the height of the Viet Nam War); and now, I suddenly had a permanent draft deferment due to my status as a father. So, while my new wife entered the first class of women at Marist to complete her final year of her degree, I managed to secure a position as a Probation Officer in the Dutchess County Probation Dept. It was there that fate stepped in.
              Although I had an uncle who was a lawyer, I was the first in my family (other than him) to go to college. I had never given thought to Law School. In my job in probation, I would often go to court on Thursday, as that was the day we would file Violation Petitions for recalcitrant probationers. I would watch the lawyers appear and plead their clients’ cases before the judges and it struck me that I was at least as smart as any of them (and probably smarter than most). So…..I took what they called the Law Boards in those days (today they call them the LSATS) and also took the exam for the NYPD. I was accepted into St. John’s Univ. Law School (then in downtown Brooklyn) and called down for my NYPD physical the same week. This was a big turning point. Although St. John’s offered me a full scholarship, they had a rule prohibiting law students from holding any kind of job, even part-time, while attending law school for 3 years. I had a wife and child to support! A boyhood friend of mine who was finishing up his first year at St. John’s Law told me that he knew that they couldn’t enforce the rule. So…..I entered law school, found a full-time job working 4 p.m. to midnight 5-6 nights a week for the Office of Probation of the City of New York as a Sr. Investigator in the Criminal Courts in Manhattan and Brooklyn. We, basically, interviewed prisoners prior to arraignment, as well as their families, employers, landlords, etc. trying to establish if they had sufficient ties to the community to recommend that they be released on their own recognizance or not. This was a tough slog for 3 years, but the job was only a short subway ride from school and we had the opportunity to study for a few hours a night on the job, during lull hours. At the end of the 3 years, I graduated #2 out of 355 in my law school class and secured a very prestigious clerkship in the Appellate Division of the New York State Supreme Court in Albany for a year.
              Nearing the end of the clerkship year in Albany, I had a lot of “sexy” (read “$$$ and prestige) offers in Albany, NYC and Wash., D.C.; however, I met the then District Attorney of Dutchess County (later Chief Admin. Judge of the State of N.Y. and Judge of the N.Y. Court of Appeals), Albert M. Rosenblatt at a social gathering. Al, who was to become my mentor, knew about my background and early achievements and told me that if I wanted to “earn my chops” as a trial lawyer, I could come on to his staff as an Asst. D.A. He promised I wouldn’t make much money (and he wasn’t lying!); but I’d get more experience than anywhere else. So…. I took a job for $5,500 a year less than I had earned in my first job out of law school and moved back to Poughkeepsie to become the Asst. D.A. assigned to the Town of Poughkeepsie Court. In that position, I handled all criminal cases from arrest through trial, from shoplifting to murder. While I had my formal office within the D.A.’s Office in the County Courthouse, I spent most of my time at the Town Court and the Town Police Station, working with the Town Justices (Jack King and Abe Banner) and the Town Police. We used to joke that I was “counsel to the Town Police”. Not that I rubber-stamped everything they did or wanted to do; but we worked very closely and consulted constantly to make airtight cases stick and to keep bad cases out of the system. I would get phone calls at all hours of the night from the detectives and the uniformed patrols with questions, etc; and that was the way I and they wanted it. On weekends, if nothing else was going on, I’d ride on patrol with them. I loved the job!
              Unfortunately, 4 ½ years after graduating I was making $13,500 and my wife and I had just purchased a first house in Millbrook for $32,500 and were hoping to have another child. One simply couldn’t support a family on an Asst. D.A.’s salary in those days. So….I joined a Poughkeepsie law firm which included Joe Spiegel, Ira Pergament, two former Asst. D.A.s and Town Justices (Ira/Town of Poughkeepsie; Joe/Town of LaGrange). I practiced there for almost 40 years, rising to the leadership position in the firm. I left the D.A.’s office and joined the firm at the beginning of 1976, when Al Rosenblatt went on the bench. At the same time, I was elected as Town Justice in the Town of Washington out in Millbrook, where I served for 4 years, (moving back to Poughkeepsie in 1980).
